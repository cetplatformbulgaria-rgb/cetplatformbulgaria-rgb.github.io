Building the Social Investment Industry in Central and Eastern Europe: The Case of Romania

The Growth and Role of Social Enterprises

Social enterprises (SEs) have emerged as a vital hybrid response to entrenched socio-economic problems such as unemployment, poverty, and social exclusion. Unlike pure market approaches or subsidized public sector programs, SEs address the root causes of disenfranchisement by creating dignified employment and providing affordable services in health, education, and renewable energy. These organizations reinvest financial returns to achieve social ends, often targeting at-risk youth, women, and people with disabilities.

Despite their growth, a significant capital gap persists. SEs often lack the financial history or collateral required by traditional commercial banks. Early-stage enterprises are particularly vulnerable due to unproven business models. There is an urgent requirement for "patient capital"—low-interest, long-term loans and quasi-equity—to transition these entities from project-based grant reliance to sustainable scaling.

The Romanian Social Enterprise Landscape

Romania faces high poverty risks, with 39.5% of the population at risk of social exclusion in 2014. Financial inclusion is the lowest in the EU, with only 60% of the population holding bank accounts. The Social Economy Atlas (2014) identifies four primary types of social economy organizations in Romania:

  • Nonprofits with economic activity (57%)
  • Retiree Mutual Organizations (31%)
  • Cooperatives (9%)
  • Private companies owned by social impact organizations (3%)

In 2015, Law 219/2015 established a legal framework for the social economy. It defined "social enterprises" and "insertion social enterprises" based on specific criteria:

  • Priority of social mission over profit.
  • Allocation of at least 90% of profits to the social mission.
  • Obligation to transfer assets to another SE upon liquidation.
  • Equitable salary levels, with a maximum 1:8 ratio between the lowest and highest pay.
  • A certification process resulting in a "social label" for entities employing at least 30% marginalized persons.

Challenges with EU Funding and Regulation

While the European Social Fund has fueled growth, it has often encouraged "project-based" thinking rather than sustainable business development. NESsT identifies several trade-offs in current EU policies:

  • Funding is often restricted to larger, established organizations.
  • Excessive focus on administrative goals rather than long-term social impact.
  • Lack of funding for intermediaries and capacity-building support.
  • Delays in national-level disbursements causing terminal cash-flow problems for small enterprises.
  • Short-term focus on job creation during the grant cycle without supporting long-term marketing or operational systems.

Financing Social Enterprise: Stages and Instruments

SE development follows a "Blueprint to Scale" model, where capital needs evolve across four stages:

  • Blueprint: Idea and launch stage. Needs: Grants and seed capital ($10k–$50k).
  • Validate: Testing the business model. Needs: Patient capital and soft loans ($50k–$300k).
  • Prepare to Scale: Achieving break-even. Needs: Quasi-equity, program-related investments, and lines of credit ($300k+).
  • Scale: Moving to new geographies. Needs: Commercial debt, impact investment, and social impact bonds ($500k+).

Technical financial instruments utilized in the sector include:

  • Recoverable Grants: Treated as grants if the enterprise fails, but must be repaid upon reaching specific milestones.
  • Program-related Investments (PRIs): Foundation-led investments with below-market returns adjusted for risk and mission.
  • Quasi-equity (Mezzanine Finance): Unsecured debt where returns are indexed to the organization’s financial performance, suitable for nonprofits that cannot issue equity.
  • Loan Guarantees: Commitments to assume debt responsibility if the borrower defaults, essential for SEs lacking collateral.
  • Social Impact Bonds (SIBs): Results-based contracts where government agencies repay investors only if specific social outcomes are achieved.

Case Study: Kék Madár Foundation (Hungary)

Kék Madár demonstrates the successful transition from grant dependency to debt financing. After launching the Ízlelő restaurant to employ persons with disabilities, the foundation required $181,700 for expansion. Despite a solid track record, commercial banks required personal collateral from the director. NESsT provided a mixed package:

  • $30,000 grant and $55,000 patient loan at 3% interest.
  • The loan was secured by a mortgage on the newly purchased building.
  • Repayment was structured in six annual installments, allowing for the refurbishment period.
  • Outcome: 100% repayment, 45 disabled people trained, and a sustainable model now ready for franchising.

Market Demand and Supply Analysis

Research into the Romanian market reveals a disconnect between demand and supply. On the demand side, 50% of surveyed SEs expressed interest in loans, yet many remain risk-averse. Key barriers include:

  • Fear of liabilities and high interest rates.
  • Lack of collateral and legal status eligibility.
  • Perception that banks do not understand the social enterprise mission.

On the supply side, financial institutions perceive SEs as high-risk due to a lack of credit history and small transaction sizes. Lenders face internal barriers, such as the absence of risk assessment tools specifically designed for social impact models. However, some regional players are innovating:

  • Romanian American Foundation (RAF): Provides bridge financing and repayable grants to mitigate currency exchange risks in EU projects.
  • BCR (Romanian Commercial Bank): Offers a social banking program with softer terms and has lent €3 million to 13 social enterprises as of 2016.
  • UniCredit Romania: Provides investment loans with maturities up to 15 years and grace periods for the principal.

Regional Investment Leaders (CEE)

Poland leads the CEE region in social finance development through several key institutions:

  • BGK (State Development Bank of Poland): Offers loans with interest rates as low as 0.44% to 0.88%, backed by EU guarantees.
  • TISE (Poland): A social loan fund managing €8 million in EaSI funding. TISE offers loans between €25,000 and €300,000 with a 50% guarantee from the European Investment Fund (EIF), specifically targeting Poland, Romania, Slovakia, and Slovenia.
  • Portus Buda Group (Hungary): A recently launched €20 million impact fund targeting for-profit and nonprofit SEs. It offers loans starting at €100,000 with 5% annual interest and requires no collateral, prioritizing measurable societal impact.

Conclusions and Strategic Recommendations

To build a robust social investment industry in Romania and the wider CEE region, the document proposes a multi-stakeholder "call to action":

  • Reform Risk Assessment: Lenders must develop tools that account for social returns and the specific cost-revenue structures of nonprofits.
  • Pilot New Instruments: Encourage the use of convertible debt and revenue-sharing schemes, often backed by philanthropic capital to absorb initial risk.
  • Support Intermediaries: Organizations like NESsT are crucial for preparing SEs to be "investment ready" through 5–7 years of engaged support.
  • Fiscal Incentives: Implement tax relief for social investors, similar to the UK’s Social Investment Tax Relief (SITR) model.
  • Public-Private Partnerships: Create funds where public capital (e.g., EU Structural Funds) mitigates the risk for private investors.
  • Standardize Metrics: Adopt industry-wide performance indicators to allow for cross-sector impact comparisons.

The establishment of the CEE Social Impact Investment Task Force marks a significant step toward catalyzing this ecosystem. By combining patient capital with business mentoring, the region can move toward meeting the UN Sustainable Development Goals while fostering a new era of inclusive economic growth.

Ashoka Romania Impact Report 2021

Organizational Mission and the "Everyone a Changemaker" Framework

Founded in 1980 by Bill Drayton, Ashoka serves as the preeminent global network for social entrepreneurs, currently supporting over 3,800 Fellows across 93 countries. The Ashoka Fellowship identifies and scales the work of social innovators who address the root causes of systemic failures rather than merely treating symptoms. Since establishing its Romanian office in 2017, the organization has focused on building an "Everyone a Changemaker" (EACH) world. This framework posits that complex social challenges require self-empowered citizens capable of leading community-level change through inclusive, non-authoritarian leadership. In Romania, Ashoka aligns international expertise with local ecosystem needs to transform structures, mindsets, and power dynamics across sectors.

Strategic Pillars for Ecosystem Development

Ashoka Romania’s operations are governed by four primary strategic pillars designed to catalyze sustainable societal change:

  • People: The rigorous identification and selection of high-impact social entrepreneurs for the Ashoka Fellows network, providing life-long support and utilizing their narratives as role models to inspire the broader ecosystem.
  • Knowledge: Addressing data deficits within the Romanian social entrepreneurship sector by producing reports, disseminating best practices, and conducting "health checks" on the state of local changemaking.
  • Values: Cultivating essential "changemaker skills" in younger generations, specifically empathy, co-creative teamwork, and fluid, team-of-teams leadership models.
  • Connected Ecosystem: Facilitating neutral collaborative spaces that bridge the gap between Ashoka Fellows, local organizations, and international stakeholders to achieve collective impact.

2021 Fellowship Cohort and Systemic Innovations

In 2021, eight Romanian social entrepreneurs were elected as Ashoka Fellows, targeting diverse systemic issues:

  • Eugen Vaida: Utilizes architectural monuments to reconstruct rural identity. By engaging citizens in safeguarding local heritage, he disrupts the pattern of rural-to-urban migration and fosters communal pride.
  • Iarina Taban: Addresses the institutional care crisis by creating a national network of "life mentors" who build long-term emotional bonds with children in state care, shifting the system's focus from physical maintenance to emotional development.
  • Paul Radu: Co-founder of the Organized Crime and Corruption Reporting Project (OCCRP), he leverages a global network of investigative journalists to fight transnational criminal organizations through collaborative data sharing.
  • Florin Stoican: Romania’s leading biodiversity advocate who creates incentive structures for businesses and individuals to engage in conservation, bridging the gap between urban centers and rural natural reserves.
  • Dorica Dan: Revolutionizes healthcare for rare disease patients by transitioning from inflexible medical structures to an integrated, collaborative service model ensuring free access to diagnostics and therapies.
  • Elena Calistru: Founder of Funky Citizens, she employs social media and non-formal education to make public information accessible, fostering a "civically fit" generation of participants in government oversight.
  • Ioana Bauer: Designs multi-stakeholder tools to disrupt human trafficking. She provides social actors with formal authority and technical skills to increase safety and institutional accountability.
  • Carmen Gheorghe: Empowers Roma women to act as bottom-up agents of change, weaving their personal insights into broader legal and societal frameworks to challenge structural inequalities and gender sub-representation.

Strategic Programs: Impact Academy and Social Finance

The Impact Academy (IA) was launched as a masterclass for civil society leaders to promote collective action over "hero-preneurship." In partnership with CSR BootIQ, the program delivered sessions on systems change, impact measurement, and cross-sector collaboration to 28 participants. Qualitative assessments indicated that the program helped leaders move from standardized metrics to vision-based system mapping.

The "Social Finance Month" initiative in October 2021 organized 9 roundtable events with 157 participants and 26 speakers. The primary objectives were to identify gaps in the Romanian social finance landscape, such as stiff legislation and lack of financial instruments. These insights informed the design of a comprehensive Social Finance Market Study aimed at accelerating the funding ecosystem starting in 2022.

Knowledge Production and The "Embracing Complexity" Report

Ashoka Romania translated and launched the Ashoka & McKinsey report, "Embracing Complexity," which details five principles for funding systems change:

  • Systems Mindset: Funders must incorporate systems change into their core DNA and actively seek transformative rather than project-based opportunities.
  • Evolving Paths: Investing in leadership capability and learning rather than strictly defined project outputs.
  • True Partnership: Actively working against power dynamics between funders and grantees to provide support that fits specific systemic needs.
  • Long-term Engagement: Acknowledging the extended timelines required for systemic shifts and allowing for strategy pivots.
  • Stakeholder Collaboration: Aligning with other funders and building networks for change leaders.

The 2021 Barometer for Changemaking, conducted with IZI Data, revealed that 8 out of 10 Romanians consider themselves changemakers, with 90% believing they have the potential to drive social change. Key factors cited for this attitude include self-education (90%) and home-based education (80%). The report noted a critical drop in confidence among the 15-24 age group, highlighting a need for targeted youth interventions. Parallel to this, the Pandemic Report emphasized that decentralized leadership and cross-sector solidarity are essential for organizational resilience during global crises.

Ashoka’s Technical Methodologies for Systems Change

The organization employs several standardized methodologies to ensure social entrepreneurs achieve systemic impact:

  • The 5R Framework: An assessment tool focusing on Results, Roles, Relationships, Rules, and Resources within a local system to identify levers for irreversible change.
  • Impact Chain Analysis: A technical mapping from Inputs and Activities to Outputs, Outcomes, and ultimate systemic Impact to identify resource gaps.
  • Collaborative Innovation Roadmap: A multi-stage process involving the forming of design teams, interviewing stakeholders, mapping strategic tensions, and developing prototypes for systemic shifts.
  • Working with Governments: A framework based on five principles: leveraging data co-creation, building civil servant capability, developing new funding models, promoting inter-sectoral collaboration, and fostering institutionalization of innovations.
